TITLE: How to Safely Use a 'Calorie Deficit' To Lose Weight
SOURCE: Cleveland Clinic
AUTHOR: Beth Czerwony, RD, LD (Registered Dietitian)
========================================================================

Losing weight is all about numbers — and we're not just talking about pounds. The formula for weight loss typically comes down to burning more calories than you're eating, a math equation called a "calorie deficit."

WHAT IS A CALORIE DEFICIT?

A "calorie deficit" means that you're taking in fewer calories through food and drinks than your body burns during the day. There are basically two ways to create a calorie deficit:
- Reduce your daily calorie intake below what you burn.
- Increase your calorie burn above what you take in.

"If you want to use a calorie deficit to lose weight, I suggest tracking the calories you're currently eating and burning before making any changes. It's good to understand your starting point." — Beth Czerwony, RD, LD

USING A CALORIE DEFICIT TO LOSE WEIGHT

A daily 500-calorie deficit should allow you to lose about 1 pound per week. Slower weight loss is more likely to stick in the long term.

"Eating 500 fewer calories per day is a good place to start if you're looking for weight loss. You can also do a smaller reduction in calorie intake — 200 or 300 calories a day — combined with an increase in exercise that burns more calories."

Even with a 500-calorie deficit, it can take your body time to adjust. You may not lose any weight for the first week or two. If you do lose weight in the first week, you're probably dropping water weight from cutting carbs and salt. After that, the fat loss begins.

HOW TO CALCULATE CALORIE DEFICIT

Use the Mifflin-Saint Jeor formula (considers height, weight, sex, age, and activity level) to estimate your daily calorie need. Then subtract your target deficit.

Example: Daily calorie need = 2,000 calories. With a 500-calorie deficit: 2,000 - 500 = 1,500 calories/day target.

RISKS OF A CALORIE DEFICIT

Taking in fewer calories than you burn can lead to: fatigue, nausea, dehydration, constipation, headaches, crabbiness. Take extra caution if you have type 2 diabetes, kidney problems, high or low blood pressure, or eating disorders. Cutting too many calories may slow your metabolism as your body conserves resources, making it harder to lose weight even while eating far less.

Outside factors that might impact your metabolism include: medications, poor sleep, stress, hormonal changes (like menopause), and underlying health conditions.

TIPS TO SEE RESULTS

- Stay hydrated — sometimes hunger is actually thirst.
- Get enough protein — limits calorie intake by making you feel fuller and maintains muscle mass.
- Eat five servings of fruits and vegetables per day.
- Consider a multivitamin to fill nutrition gaps.
- Talk to a healthcare provider — weight loss is complex, and professional guidance helps.
